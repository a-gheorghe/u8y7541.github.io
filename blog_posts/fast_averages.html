Fast Mental Math Averages

When most people are asked to calculate the average of $\{5, 5, 5, 6, 6, 6, 6\}$ in their head, they start doing the long, arduous process of adding and dividing which we are taught in school. It's standard to compute $\frac{5 + 5 + 5 + 6 + 6 + 6 + 6}{7}$, and it will take a minute to get the answer. This can be annoying sometimes, especially in math competitions where time is very important. Fortunately, there is a better way to calculate averages by mental math.

One way to calculate averages fast is to think that each number is a person. All of the same numbers are put on a team. The two teams play tug of war. In the case above, there would be three people on the $5$ team, pulling the rope their way, and 4 people on the $6$ team.

If we attached a sign to the middle of the line, after the two teams pulled, the position of the sign would be the average. Using intuition, because there are four people on the $6$ team and three people on the $5$ team, the average ("sign") should be $\frac{4}{7}$ of the way between 5 and 6. In our heads, we can clearly see that $\frac{4}{7}$ of the way between 5 and 6 is $\boxed{5\frac{4}{7}}$. If you don't believe me, you can check by calculating the traditional method!

Compacting into a formula

This method's nice, but it's easier to express it as a formula. After some work, I got:

$$\frac{ab + cd}{a + c} = \frac{a}{a + c} \times (b - d) + d$$

An example use:

$\begin{align}\frac{2 \times 20 + 3 \times 30}{2 + 3} = \frac{2}{2 + 3} \times (20 - 30) + 30 = \frac{2}{5} \times -10 + 30 = -4 + 30 = \boxed{26}\end{align}$

That formula looks ugly, so don't use it. It's easier just to remember the "tug of war" scenario.

Other methods

"But isn't there something more faster?" you say. There is one. And that involves using your brain and intuition.

Suppose you need to find the average of $\{5, 5, 5, 5, 7, 7, 7, 10\}$. We could set out using the formula above twice to get the right answer, or we could use some shortcuts. Three fives and three sevens would average out to $6$. The remaining $5$ would average with $11$, producing $8$. So now we need to find the average of $\{6, 6, 6, 6, 6, 6, 8, 8\}$. Because there are six $6$s and two $8$s, we can reduce it to three $6$s and one $8$. Then it is even easier to solve, and we can do it with the previous method and obtain $6.25$.

Conclusion

In the end, it's just what you find works for you best. Remember, I found all of these out by myself. It seems easy to me, but some people think what I do is too complicated. Have fun beating your friends at math, and wait for the next post!
